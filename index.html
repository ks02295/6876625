Indroduction

In the last 100 years, we have explored the many wonders of our universe – from the precise orbit of the planets in our solar system to the furthest galaxy away from us. 
  However, all the matter we can see and have researched on only makes up 5% (Meserve, 2014) of how much we truly have explored it. One of the biggest mysteries that scientists are 
  struggling to solve is the existence of dark matter and dark energy where dark matter involves gravity to form galaxies/galaxy clusters by sticking gas, dust and stars 
  together (NASA Space Place, 2024) while dark energy is involved in accelerating the expansion of the universe (Lea, 2022).  This will be further discussed throughout where we
  see how these two ideas have come to be, how we can detect dark matter and energy and if there’s any technology we can use to strengthen the evidence around these two mysterious substances.

How was dark matter and energy discovered?

Dark matter was first thought of in the early 1930s (Bautista, no date) by a Swiss American astronomer called Fritz Zwicky, who realised that the stars making up the Coma Cluster of galaxies only made up 1% of 
  the mass needed to keep the galaxies from escaping the gravitational pull (Riess, 2024). Decades later during the 1970s (Riess, 2024), an astronomer named Vera Rubin confirmed that dark matter did exist by
  looking at the velocities of the inner and outer stars of the Andromeda Galaxy. However, they realised that the rotation was flat – the velocities of the stars at the centre and at the outer ends of the galaxy 
  were the same. It was surprising because the outer stars were moving so fast that it should have flown away. This then concluded that there was some kind of matter which we can’t see that was holding the galaxy 
  together – this was dark matter (Childers, 2019).
Dark energy, despite the name, isn’t the same as dark matter. Dark energy accelerates the expansion of the universe and works against gravity to tear stars and galaxies apart (Lea, 2022). 
  It was discovered when two independent astronomers studied the explosion of white dwarfs (called type 1a supernova) where they were able to see the emitted light coming from the blast but the further they
  were away from them, the dimmer the light coming from the blasts will be. The distance was then measured and found out that the supernovas with greater redshifts had dimmer light then it should be where the
  expansion of the universe was not speeding up. Therefore, they concluded that it was some sort of dark energy that was accelerating the expansion of the universe (NASA Science, 2023).
